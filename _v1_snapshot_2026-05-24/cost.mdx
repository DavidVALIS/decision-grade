## Four lines to take to your board

<Steps>
  <Step title="We are already paying for verification, just inefficiently.">
    Either as an explicit review-team line item, or as the unmeasured cost of errors that slip through. Both sit in our budget; only one is currently visible.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The question is not whether to verify. It is how.">
    Humans at independent-grade review cost about $165 per artifact. An automated multi-family stack costs about $40. Same job. Better catch rate, because three independent model families share fewer blind spots than two pairs of human eyes.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The savings scale with what we produce.">
    Roughly $250,000 a year on 2,000 briefings. More than $1 million a year on 300 longform reports. The stack scales with volume; humans scale with headcount.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The downside is bounded. The upside is the cost of one bad call.">
    A single uncaught error in a board memo or regulatory filing can cost six or seven figures. The first time the stack catches one that would have reached a binding decision, it has paid for itself.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## The follow-up questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="How were these numbers derived?">
    Marginal labour minutes at loaded analyst and supervisor rates, multi-family compute at API pricing, fixed-cost amortisation by annual volume. The full cost function, parameter set, and break-even math are in the [open workbook](/verification_stack_cost_model.xlsx). Every input is editable. Replace any default with your own measured number and the entire model re-runs.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Why does the stack catch more errors than humans, not fewer?">
    Cognitive orthogonality. Three different model families share fewer training overlaps, cultural reflexes, and analytical blind spots than two humans on the same team, however senior. A second human reader is a different pair of eyes; three model families are three different reasoning chains. At independent-grade review the stack catches around 35% of structural errors; a human pair catches around 28%. The gap widens at higher tiers.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="When does the comparison NOT favour the stack?">
    Three places. Novel domains the models haven't seen — human catch rate rises and the comparison reverses. Regulated industries where human review is policy — the regulator decides, not the economics. And organisations producing fewer than ~200 decision-grade artifacts a year — fixed costs don't amortise and the stack is honestly more expensive. In all three cases the workbook will recommend against the stack. That's the falsifier working.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What about defensibility — cryptographic trails, regulatory exposure?">
    Available at the top verification tier for around $8,000 a year of additional fixed cost. Pays for itself the first time discovery reaches your reasoning chain. Humans cannot produce equivalent tamper-evident reasoning trails at scale; that property is uniquely a stack capability.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What does the verification stack actually do, in operational terms?">
    Runs multiple independent model families on every output, flags disagreements for focused human adjudication, applies architectural gates and a rubric, requires supervisor signoff, and (at the top tier) issues a cryptographically anchored certificate. The long-form treatment is in [The Doctrine](/the-doctrine) and [the Buyer's Checklist](/buyers-checklist).
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What's the downside if we adopt and we're wrong?">
    The stack's fixed costs are modest ($30k–$60k a year). Compute is metered, not contracted. Every parameter is open; the model will recommend the cheaper option — humans, or no verification at all — the moment your numbers justify it. If you change your mind, you turn it off. There is no 2-year contract or 6-person team to dissolve.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What if my organisation is regulated and human review is mandatory?">
    The stack still pays. Adjudication is human, supervisor signoff is human, audit sampling is human, refusal-log review is human. The stack replaces the *line-by-line reading* that humans do worse than orthogonal models, not the judgment work humans uniquely do well. Regulators care about whether qualified humans signed off; they don't care that a stack drafted the rubric the humans applied.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
