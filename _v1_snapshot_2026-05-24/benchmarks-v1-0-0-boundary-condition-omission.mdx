title: 'Can it notice what is missing?'
description: 'Boundary condition omission. 500 cases. All three frontier models scored below 0.41 macro F1. The wedge furthest from the decision-grade bar.'
---

The test presents an analysis that is internally coherent and appears complete but omits a boundary condition a careful reader would catch. The cases are constructed so the omission is detectable from the text alone, without outside knowledge. The model is asked to surface the omission. The score is macro F1 against the constructed truth set.

<BenchmarkTestSection wedge="boundary_condition_omission" name="The numbers" scoreLine="Macro F1 · 500 cases · 3 models">

Across 500 cases per model, Claude Opus 4.7 scored macro F1 0.40. GPT-5.5 scored 0.32. Gemini 3.1 Pro scored 0.32. Three frontier reasoning models, three independent providers, three different training corpora. The result is consistent.

Recall on the truth set ranges from 0.43 to 0.55 across the three models. Precision ranges from 0.25 to 0.34. When the model flags an omission, it is correct in approximately one third of cases; when an omission exists, the model surfaces it in approximately half of cases. The pattern is consistent across providers and across case difficulty.

The directional finding: frontier reasoning models evaluate what is present in an analysis with higher reliability than they evaluate what is absent. The completeness check is the weaker capability.

## What would falsify this finding

Any of the three frontier models reaching macro F1 above 0.60 at the v1.0.0 case construction. None do at this version.

A natural critique is that v1.0.0 uses a bare, one-shot prompt and that sophisticated prompt scaffolding might close the gap. Whether sophisticated prompting moves boundary-condition omission from 0.40 to 0.60 or higher is an open question. v1.1 of the benchmarks programme is scoped to test this directly.
